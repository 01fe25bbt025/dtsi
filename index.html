The Beginning

We thought we knew the problem.

We arrived at the Anganwadi on 08 March 2026 with four assumptions about what we would find — families not feeding their children well, parents who did not follow health advice, and a community that needed awareness. The community kept correcting us, whenever we got derailed from the actual problem.

Step 01 · 08 March 2026
Visit 1 — What We Saw

One teacher managing 20–30 children alone. Charts on the wall she had bought herself. Children learning on chalk and slate. A single laddoo served as the mid-morning meal, and rice/sambar for lunchmost children pushed it aside. Government ration supplies had been declining for 2–3 years.

"We always give proper food at home, it is here the food is less."

— Mother, Anganwadi
Step 02 · Fieldwork
Indirect Questioning — Listening Differently

We designed indirect questions to understand how the community actually lives — not to confirm what we expected. We asked mothers to walk us through what their child ate from the moment they woke up. The answers showed us the family was never the problem.

Home meals: rice, dal, roti, sambar
Center meal: one laddoo only
Worker: zero control over food supplied
Teacher self-funded all class materials
Step 03 · Pilot Visit
The Recipe Book — Testing Our Idea

We first considered a nutrition chart. The worker told us they had tried charts before — nobody used them, they just collected dust. So we made a small illustrated recipe book in Kannada instead — nutritious meals using only basic home ingredients. When we gave it to the worker, she said it would be perfect for the nutrition briefing sessions she runs for parents when a child turns 3. A moment we had not even known existed.

"We serve what the government sends. We cannot buy or change items ourselves."

— Anganwadi Worker
Step 04 · Reflection
Three Corrections. Three Better Decisions.

We started with a chart. The worker said charts collect dust. We moved to a recipe book. We planned to give it to mothers. We could not reach them. We gave it to the worker. She showed us a briefing session we did not know existed. Every correction made the intervention better than what we had designed.

The problem was never the family. It was always the system.

The ICDS ration supply chain fails at the last mile. The worker has no autonomy. The teacher funds her own classroom. And yet the community — quietly, without complaint — absorbs every gap the system leaves behind.
